Project Statement

There is power in paying attention, and this is the resounding takeaway I have from reading Unmasking AI: My Mission to Protect What Is Human in a World of Machines by Joy Buolamwini. This book is a beautifully written critique of technology and a call for readers to think about how power, identity, and responsibility are embedded in the systems we design and rely upon. From the title alone, we are reminded that as humans who create technology, our humanity is shaped by that technology. Artificial intelligence is laced with human influence and political constructs.

Three lessons resonated with me the most: the concept of the coded gaze and the myth of neutral defaults, the role of observation in advocacy, and the collective responsibility we have to fight for algorithmic justice. Early on, readers are introduced to the “coded gaze,” the notion that technology often reflects the perspectives, priorities, and blind spots of those who design it. Buolamwini emphasizes that “defaults are not neutral,” illustrating this through her work at MIT with facial recognition systems that failed to recognize her dark-skinned face.

Too often, technological systems are framed as objective or purely technical. Training datasets and design choices are treated as intuitive or inevitable, when in reality, they privilege certain identities and experiences over others. The idea of a “default user” is frequently implicitly white, male, Western, and able-bodied. When these defaults go unquestioned, harm becomes systemic rather than accidental. Buolamwini calls on readers to interrogate these assumptions critically.

Knowledge empowers us, and much can be learned through observation. Rather than framing advocacy as purely oppositional, Buolamwini highlights the importance of paying close attention to how systems operate in practice. Her “Gender Shades” project exemplifies this approach by combining data with narrative, artistic expression, and lived experience, making disparities legible and concrete.

This methodology bridges technical expertise and social understanding. By centering empathy, conversations about accountability and responsibility become more accessible and more complete. Buolamwini’s engagement with public policy, including founding the Algorithmic Justice League and testifying before Congress, demonstrates how observation can translate into meaningful policy action, such as influencing the 2022 Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights.

Finally, Buolamwini reinforces that justice must be a collective pursuit. Algorithmic bias cannot be viewed as distant or irrelevant. As a member of marginalized communities, indifference is not an option. Technology has often mirrored historical attempts at erasure, and resisting that erasure requires awareness, action, and courage.

Through her journey, Buolamwini reminds us that AI systems can be challenged, reshaped, and governed more justly. Unmasking AI offers a vision of possibility and optimism grounded in education, attentiveness, and collective responsibility. Ultimately, this book has taught me to question system defaults, to notice the significance of the technologies around me, and to make those observations actionable. The future of AI is not predetermined—it is something we are collectively authoring.
